1. What Are Stable Isotopes?

Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. This means they have identical chemical properties but slightly different masses.

üîë Key Definition

Stable isotopes are nuclides that do not undergo radioactive decay. Unlike radiogenic isotopes (e.g., ¬π‚Å¥C, ¬≤¬≥‚Å∏U), stable isotopes remain unchanged over geological time, making them ideal tracers of past environmental conditions.

Why Do Mass Differences Matter?

Although isotopes of an element behave almost identically in chemical reactions, their mass differences cause subtle variations in:

  • Reaction rates ‚Äî lighter isotopes react slightly faster
  • Bond strengths ‚Äî heavier isotopes form stronger bonds
  • Diffusion rates ‚Äî lighter isotopes diffuse faster
  • Vapor pressures ‚Äî lighter isotopes evaporate more readily

These differences lead to isotope fractionation ‚Äî the partitioning of isotopes between different phases or compounds during physical and chemical processes.

3. Isotope Fractionation

Fractionation occurs because isotopes partition differently between phases or during reactions. There are two main types:

A. Equilibrium Fractionation

‚öñÔ∏è Equilibrium (Exchange) Fractionation

  • Occurs in reversible reactions at thermodynamic equilibrium
  • Heavy isotopes preferentially concentrate in the more strongly bonded phase
  • Fractionation decreases with increasing temperature
  • Example: Oxygen isotope exchange between water and calcite

At equilibrium, the heavier isotope concentrates in the phase where it forms the strongest bonds. For carbonates precipitating from water:

B. Kinetic Fractionation

üèÉ Kinetic Fractionation

  • Occurs in irreversible, unidirectional processes
  • Light isotopes react and diffuse faster
  • Common in: evaporation, diffusion, biological reactions (photosynthesis)
  • Often produces larger fractionations than equilibrium processes

A key principle in isotope geochemistry is that equilibrium fractionation decreases with increasing temperature. At infinite temperature, isotopes would distribute randomly with no fractionation. This temperature dependence is the foundation of isotope paleothermometry.

5. Measuring Isotope Ratios

Stable isotope ratios are measured using gas-source isotope ratio mass spectrometry (IRMS).

Basic Principle

  1. Sample preparation ‚Äî Convert sample to gas (CO‚ÇÇ for C and O isotopes)

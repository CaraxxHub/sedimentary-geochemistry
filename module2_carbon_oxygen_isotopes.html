1.3 Interpreting Carbon Isotope Excursions

üìà Positive Œ¥13C Excursions

Cause: Enhanced burial of organic carbon (Corg)

Mechanism: When more organic carbon is buried, 12C is preferentially removed from the ocean ‚Üí residual DIC becomes enriched in 13C ‚Üí carbonates precipitated during this time have higher Œ¥13C values.

Evidence: Often associated with Corg-rich sediments (black shales)

Climatic link: May indicate CO2 drawdown ‚Üí potential cooling

üìâ Negative Œ¥13C Excursions

  • Input of isotopically light carbon (volcanic CO2 or CH4)
  • Oxidation of organic matter reservoirs

Example: The Permo-Triassic boundary shows a ~3.5‚Ä∞ negative excursion linked to Siberian Traps volcanism and potential release of thermogenic methane.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Parallel Shifts in Œ¥13Ccarb and Œ¥13Corg

When ocean Œ¥13CDIC changes, both carbonate and organic carbon records show the same excursions (parallel shifts). This is because:

  • Marine carbonates record Œ¥13C of DIC directly
  • Organic matter inherits the DIC signal, offset by photosynthetic fractionation

This parallel behavior confirms a primary ocean signal rather than diagenetic alteration.

3. The Ice Volume Effect

3.1 How Ice Sheets Affect Seawater Œ¥18O

When water evaporates from the ocean and precipitates at high latitudes, 16O is preferentially incorporated into precipitation and ice. This creates a significant reservoir of isotopically light water stored in ice sheets.

4. Diagenetic Alteration

4.1 Recognizing Diagenesis

Diagenesis can significantly alter primary isotope signals. Recognizing and avoiding diagenetically altered samples is critical for reliable paleoenvironmental reconstructions.

üî¨ Screening Methods for Diagenesis

  • Cathodoluminescence (CL): Non-luminescent (black) = well-preserved; bright = altered
  • Trace elements: Elevated Mn, Fe and low Sr indicate alteration
  • SEM examination: Original microstructure preserved vs. recrystallized
  • Œ¥18O-Œ¥13C covariation: Strong correlation suggests diagenetic overprinting

  • Dissolving marine carbonate (Œ¥13C ‚âà 0 to +4‚Ä∞)

Low-magnesium calcite shells (brachiopods, belemnites) are preferred for paleoclimate studies because:

3. The Phanerozoic Seawater Sr Curve

The ‚Å∏‚Å∑Sr/‚Å∏‚Å∂Sr ratio of seawater has varied significantly through the Phanerozoic:

  • Minimum: Middle Jurassic (~0.7068)
  • Maximum: Present day (~0.7092)
  • Major rises: Late Ordovician, Late Paleozoic, Cenozoic

4. The Cenozoic Rise: A Case Study

The most dramatic feature of the Sr curve is the continuous rise over the last 40 million years from ~0.7077 to ~0.7092.

üèîÔ∏è The Himalayan Connection

The collision of India with Asia (~50 Ma) and subsequent Himalayan uplift exposed:

  • Old, highly radiogenic rocks (‚Å∏‚Å∑Sr/‚Å∏‚Å∂Sr > 0.720)
  • High-grade metamorphic terranes
  • Ancient granites with high Rb/Sr ratios

Rivers draining the Himalayas have exceptionally high ‚Å∏‚Å∑Sr/‚Å∏‚Å∂Sr:

  • Ganges: ~0.725 (much higher than global river average of ~0.711)
  • Brahmaputra: ~0.720
  • These rivers deliver disproportionately radiogenic Sr to the ocean

5. Sr Isotope Stratigraphy: Dating Applications

Because seawater ‚Å∏‚Å∑Sr/‚Å∏‚Å∂Sr changed through time, it can be used for chronostratigraphic dating.

üìÖ How It Works

  1. Measure ‚Å∏‚Å∑Sr/‚Å∏‚Å∂Sr of well-preserved marine carbonate
  2. Compare to the global seawater Sr curve (McArthur et al., 2020)
  3. Find the age(s) where the measured ratio intersects the curve

Well-preserved sample Diagenesis can alter original ‚Å∏‚Å∑Sr/‚Å∏‚Å∂Sr

Good for dating (steep gradients):

  • Cenozoic (especially Neogene): excellent resolution
  • Late Cretaceous: good resolution
  • Early Triassic: steep rise after PTB

Poor for dating (flat or reversing):

  • Middle Jurassic: minimum, flat curve
  • Cambrian: limited data, possible diagenesis

Under optimal conditions:

  • Cenozoic: ¬±0.5-1 million years
  • Late Mesozoic: ¬±1-2 million years
  • Paleozoic: ¬±2-5 million years (curve less well constrained)

6. Case Study: Permo-Triassic Boundary

üìà Sharp Rise Across the PTB

‚Å∏‚Å∑Sr/‚Å∏‚Å∂Sr increases sharply from ~0.7070 to ~0.7082 across the Permo-Triassic boundary and into the Early Triassic.

Traditional Interpretation (Algeo & Twitchett, 2010)

The Sr isotope rise has been interpreted as evidence for:

  • Enhanced continental silicate weathering
  • Negative feedback on atmospheric CO‚ÇÇ
  • Mechanism for post-extinction recovery

‚ö†Ô∏è Alternative Interpretation

The Sr isotope signal could also reflect:

  • Change in what is being weathered (more radiogenic source rocks)
  • Drainage reorganization exposing different lithologies

This is an active area of research ‚Äî provenance changes vs. intensity changes.
